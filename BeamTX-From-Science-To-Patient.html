From Science to Patient

A Strategic Approach to Scaling BEAM's Gene Editing Pipeline.

BEAM’s base editing platform has the potential to deliver lifelong cures. Success hinges not only on technical precision—it requires patient trust, equitable access, and readiness across site networks to deliver these therapies effectively. The Challenge Overview

While the science is groundbreaking, the last mile to the patient is the most delicate and mission-critical phase. These are the primary bottlenecks in patient-centric delivery for BEAM.

Delivery Complexity

*Ex vivo* and *in vivo* modalities demand different practitioner skill sets, infrastructure, and coordination, creating significant operational challenges.

Practitioner Education Gaps

Specialty centers may be new to base editing workflows, including conditioning regimens, LNP infusion protocols, and unique long-term follow-up requirements.

Patient Identification

Rare disease populations are dispersed and often unaware of trial options, with higher barriers to entry existing in underserved communities.

Diversity & Inclusion Mandates

Upcoming FDA and EU regulations require proactive, documented strategies for inclusive recruitment, making diversity a regulatory necessity.

Long-Term Retention

Multi-year tracking is essential to prove the durability of "curative" claims, but patient fatigue and disengagement risk are high over such long periods.

Why This Matters Now

As BEAM advances, patient access and operational inclusivity will directly impact regulatory success, payer acceptance, and market adoption.

A Patient-Centered Operational Framework

This framework is designed to proactively address each challenge with a specific, measurable, and patient-focused set of initiatives.

A. Education & Readiness

Practitioner Bootcamps: On-site and virtual training modules on *ex vivo* logistics, *in vivo* infusion safety, and decentralized monitoring tools.
Patient-Friendly Materials: Co-create plain-language explanations of base editing, leveraging patient advocacy groups to build trust and comprehension.
Community Touchpoints: Partner with patient organizations, rare disease coalitions, and local health systems to create “educational champions” in the community.

B. Diversity & DCT Integration

Diversity Outreach Map: Identify trial site catchment areas against demographic disease prevalence data to ensure representative access.
Decentralized Trial Elements: Use remote consent, telemedicine, and mobile phlebotomy for stable patients to reduce travel burden.
Culturally Tailored Recruitment: Partner with minority physician networks, advocacy groups, and faith-based health outreach initiatives.

C. Strategic Site & Advocacy Relationships

Site Relationship Model: Build strong operational and advocacy ties with KOLs, site coordinators, and community liaisons beyond standard sponsor-CRO interactions.
Embedded Patient Advocates: Place trained patient navigators in high-enrollment sites to support trial retention and act as a bridge between patients and the study team.

D. Retention & Long-Term Follow-Up

Retention Scorecard: Monitor engagement risk factors per patient (e.g., missed appointments) and deploy interventions before disengagement occurs.
Engagement Rewards: Recognize patient milestones (e.g., anniversary of treatment) in ethical, regulatory-compliant ways to maintain connection and show appreciation.
